# Screenplay Discipline

Use this reference during Stage 2 to create `production/02_research/screenplay_discipline.md`. Stage 3 must follow the approved screenplay discipline when producing chapter architecture.

## Scene Contract

Every planned scene should define:

- `scene_id`
- chapter
- location
- time of day
- weather state
- POV owner
- conflict
- scene turn
- new information
- emotional change
- visual anchor
- language tags
- continuity dependencies
- safety/sensitivity constraints
- exit hook

Do not move to screenplay drafting if chapter architecture lacks scene-level purpose and turn logic.

## POV Discipline

Assign an emotional owner to each scene. The protagonist may be central, but not every scene should be limited to what the protagonist understands.

Track scenes owned by:

- protagonist,
- local political/moral anchor,
- institutional counterpart,
- field ally or pursuer,
- civilian witness,
- antagonist or antagonist system.

If only the protagonist makes decisive choices, flag outsider-savior drift.

## Information Release

Maintain a reveal ledger:

- what the audience knows,
- what the protagonist knows,
- what the institution knows,
- what local allies know,
- what antagonists know,
- when each knowledge state changes.

Avoid dialogue that explains what the camera, blocking, consequence, or prior scene already showed.

## Subtext

Themes should live primarily in choices, refusals, silence, physical cost, and consequences. Avoid direct speeches about democracy, heroism, suffering, duty, or justice unless the scene has earned them.

## Dialogue And Language

Before drafting dialogue, define voice rules per major character:

- sentence length,
- humor style,
- emotional leakage,
- forbidden phrases,
- pressure behavior,
- code-switching rules,
- sample safe lines.

Every multilingual scene must label language intent, such as:

- `[in formal Bengali]`
- `[in Chittagonian; subtitled]`
- `[in Rohingya; subtitled]`
- `[in Rakhine; subtitled]`
- `[in Burmese; subtitled]`
- `[in English]`

Do not fake dialect through comic misspelling. Use rhythm, syntax, code-switching, and reviewed vocabulary.

## Action Prose

Action lines should be cinematic, readable, and consequence-focused. Show danger, geography, fear, confusion, exhaustion, and moral cost.

Do not include procedural detail for:

- weapon optimization,
- drone modification,
- explosive design,
- border evasion,
- ambush planning,
- radio relay setup,
- communications disruption.

## Continuity Ledger

Track at minimum:

- weather,
- mud and water transfer,
- wounds and fatigue,
- wardrobe state,
- props,
- vehicle condition,
- time of day,
- language comprehension,
- geography,
- emotional state,
- promises and secrets,
- setup/payoff dependencies.

## Moral Agency Check

For every chapter, identify decisive choices made by non-protagonist characters. Local characters and civilians must affect outcomes, not only explain stakes or suffer consequences.

## Draft Pass Order

Use separate passes:

1. beats,
2. scene cards,
3. rough screenplay,
4. dialogue pass,
5. action/geography pass,
6. language pass,
7. sensitivity pass,
8. continuity pass.

Do not treat a first generated screenplay as final prose.
